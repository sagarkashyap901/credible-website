New War Erupts in the Middle East: Saudi Arabia and Houthis Attack Each Other

A dispute over one Iranian aircraft carrying a Houthi delegation home from Tehran has unraveled Yemen's longest period of calm since 2015, pulling Pakistan's new defense pact with Riyadh into an active theater for the first time.

The Yemeni government and Iran-backed Houthi rebels exchanged direct strikes on Monday, July 13, ending a de facto truce that had held — despite never being formally renewed — since April 2022. The clash marks the most serious military escalation between the two sides in four years, and unfolds against a Gulf security landscape already destabilized by the broader Iran-US confrontation.

No casualties were reported on either side. But the symbolism is significant: it is the first time Houthi forces have struck Saudi territory, and the first time the Yemeni government has struck Sanaa, since the 2022 truce took effect.

What happened, in sequence

  1. The trigger. A senior Houthi delegation traveled to Tehran to attend the funeral of Iran's late supreme leader. Yemen's internationally recognized government pressed the delegation to fly home aboard Yemenia, the national carrier, rather than an Iranian aircraft. When that effort failed, Yemen's defense ministry said its forces would confront any "hostile aircraft" violating Yemeni sovereignty "by all available means."
  2. The first strike. Government forces struck the runway at Sanaa International Airport on Monday, aiming to prevent the Iranian aircraft from landing. Yemen's defense minister, Gen. Taher al-Aqili, said the government's patience had run out and framed the strike as a sovereignty issue rather than an act of war against the Houthis directly. The Iranian aircraft was diverted and landed instead at Hodeida Airport, in Houthi-controlled territory.
  3. Yemen closes its skies. Authorities aligned with the Yemeni government declared all of the country's airports closed until further notice.
  4. The Houthi retaliation. Hours later, Houthi forces fired ballistic missiles and drones at Abha International Airport in southwestern Saudi Arabia. Houthi military spokesman Yahya Saree said the operation met its objectives and warned that Saudi Arabia would bear responsibility for further escalation. Saree also cautioned airlines against flying through Saudi airspace until what he called the blockade on Sanaa airport is lifted — a warning with direct implications for commercial aviation risk in the region.
  5. Saudi air defenses respond. A spokesperson for the Saudi-led coalition said air defenses intercepted the ballistic missile threat toward the kingdom's southern region, without disclosing further operational detail.
  6. The UN steps in. The UN Security Council convened an emergency session the same day. A senior UN political affairs official warned that the region cannot afford another cycle of escalation and called on all parties to return to UN-mediated talks.

Why the truce held for four years — and why it broke now

Yemen's civil war began in 2014, when Houthi forces seized Sanaa and forced the internationally recognized government into exile. A Saudi-led coalition, including the UAE, intervened in 2015 to restore the government — triggering a war that the UN estimates has caused roughly 377,000 deaths, the majority from indirect causes such as blockade-driven starvation and collapsed healthcare.

An April 2022 UN-brokered truce halted large-scale coalition airstrikes and reopened limited commercial flights from Sanaa. The truce was never formally renewed after October 2022, yet both sides largely observed it in practice — until Monday.

Three structural cracks had already widened before this week's flashpoint:

  • December 2025: A power struggle between the Yemeni government and the Southern Transitional Council turned violent in oil-rich southern territory, forcing Saudi Arabia to intervene on the government's side against its own coalition partner.
  • January 2026: The UAE withdrew its counter-terrorism forces from Yemen after a Saudi strike hit UAE-backed units — exposing fractures inside the anti-Houthi coalition itself.
  • Early July 2026: Yemeni government forces and Houthi fighters clashed near Hodeida in the most violent confrontation in years, days before the airport strikes.

Iran's expanding footprint in Sanaa — symbolized by direct flights between Tehran and Houthi-held territory — is the thread connecting all of it. Riyadh has treated any Iranian physical presence in Yemen as a direct threat since the war began, and the funeral flight dispute simply gave that long-standing anxiety a concrete trigger.

The Pakistan variable

This is the first Houthi-Saudi flare-up to occur since Saudi Arabia and Pakistan signed their Strategic Mutual Defence Agreement (SMDA) in September 2025 — a pact under which aggression against either country is to be treated as aggression against both.

The pact is not theoretical. Earlier this year, amid the separate Iran-Israel-US confrontation, Saudi Arabia invoked the SMDA and Pakistan deployed roughly 8,000 troops, 16 aircraft, and two drone squadrons to Saudi soil. Separately, Pakistan's 25th Mechanised Division — roughly 10,000 desert-trained troops equipped with tanks and self-propelled artillery — has already been stationed near the Saudi-Yemen border specifically to help counter Houthi threats.

Pakistan's obligations under the SMDA are unlikely to translate into ground troops fighting inside Yemen; Islamabad refused exactly that request from Riyadh in 2015.

That existing posture matters for what comes next. Analysts note that Pakistan's obligations under the SMDA are unlikely to translate into ground troops fighting inside Yemen; Islamabad refused exactly that request from Riyadh in 2015. More probable forms of support include reinforced air defense assistance, intelligence sharing, and protection of Saudi infrastructure — missions Pakistani forces are already positioned to perform.

What to watch

  • Whether Iran-Sanaa flights resume or stop entirely — the next such flight will be a direct test of whether either side is willing to de-escalate.
  • Houthi follow-through on the airspace warning — a strike or attempted strike against commercial aviation would mark a sharp further escalation.
  • Pakistani troop posture near the Yemen border — any visible reinforcement would signal the SMDA is being operationalized specifically for this front, not just the Iran front.
  • UN-mediated talks — the Security Council's call for negotiation is the only de-escalation track currently on the table.
