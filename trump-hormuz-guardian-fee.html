Trump Declares U.S. 'Guardian' of Hormuz, Reimposes Iran Blockade, Demands Payment for Securing the Strait

Washington's proposal to charge for protection of the world's most critical oil chokepoint has no legal precedent, no defined collection mechanism, and no clear enforcement date — but it has already moved oil markets and reopened a fight the U.S. spent months insisting it would never wage.

President Donald Trump declared Monday that the United States will be known "from this point forward" as the Guardian of the Hormuz Strait, and said Washington will collect a fee equal to 20% of the value of all cargo shipped through the waterway to cover the cost of securing it. In the same Truth Social post, Trump said the U.S. is reinstating its naval blockade of Iranian ports and vessels — six weeks after a memorandum of understanding was supposed to have wound that blockade down.

The announcement lands in the middle of the sharpest exchange of fire between Washington and Tehran since a ceasefire framework was signed in mid-June. It also marks a reversal: the U.S. spent the better part of this year rejecting Iran's own attempts to charge vessels for safe passage through the Strait, on the grounds that tolls on an international waterway have no basis in maritime law. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said as recently as last month that no country is permitted to charge fees for transiting an international strait. Washington's new proposal does not repeal that argument — it simply proposes to be the one collecting.

The trigger: a weekend of escalation

The blockade announcement did not arrive in isolation. Trump declared the ceasefire "over" last week after Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps resumed strikes on commercial shipping. Over the weekend, the IRGC struck a container vessel near the strait's western entrance and declared the waterway "closed until further notice," prompting two rounds of U.S. airstrikes on Iranian targets Saturday and Sunday. Iran's Persian Gulf Strait Authority said Monday that transit through Hormuz was "currently unfeasible" given recent U.S. military action. The IRGC, in turn, struck targets in Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait and Oman — the broadest regional exchange since the earlier ceasefire took hold.

MarineTraffic data show vessel activity through the strait fell roughly 52% between July 10 and July 12 compared with the prior week. Brent crude rose more than 3% on Monday on the disruption, and U.S. equity indexes fell.

What Trump said, and what he didn't

Trump's Truth Social post framed the arrangement as a matter of "FAIRNESS": the U.S. has patrolled the Strait for decades, he argued, without ever being compensated, while other nations profited from safe passage. Speaking earlier Monday on Fox News, he was more informal, suggesting the U.S. might brand the arrangement the "Guardian Angel of the Strait" and predicting wealthy Gulf nations would ultimately bear the cost.

What the post did not include is any detail on implementation. The White House did not respond to press questions Monday on how the fee would be calculated, who would collect it, or when it would take effect beyond Trump's assurance that "the process and formation will begin immediately."

Maritime law and shipping analysts contacted by U.S. outlets offered three distinct readings of what a 20% "reimbursement" could actually mean in practice:

  • A cost-recovery levy, splitting the U.S. Navy's blockade and escort expenses across transiting vessels — a model raised by John McCown, a senior fellow at the Center for Maritime Strategy, who noted even this framing leaves the calculation method undefined.
  • A percentage-of-cargo-value surcharge, which would dwarf conventional tolls charged at chokepoints such as the Suez or Panama canals, both of which price by vessel tonnage rather than cargo value.
  • A voluntary convoy fee, under which the Navy would escort ships on an opt-in basis — the only version, according to James Kraska, a professor of international maritime law at the U.S. Naval War College, that would likely satisfy international law, since it would not condition the underlying right of transit on payment.

That legal distinction is central to the story. The Strait of Hormuz is classified as a strait used for international navigation, where vessels hold a right of free passage that cannot lawfully be made contingent on a fee. That is the same principle Washington invoked to reject Iran's toll plans earlier in the conflict.

Iran's response: rejection, then ridicule

Iran's reaction unfolded on two tracks. Its military command flatly rejected Trump's claim of U.S. authority over the strait, saying Washington has no role in determining its future. Foreign ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei said the June framework underpinning months of negotiation was now "in crisis."

Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi took a sharper, more rhetorical approach on X, agreeing with Trump's underlying premise while turning it against him:

"POTUS is absolutely right. Whoever provides secure and safe passage of commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz should be compensated for this service. Iran has always been the GUARDIAN of the Strait and will remain so FOREVER. 20% is of course too much. We will be fair."

The message effectively reasserted Iran's own long-standing claim to toll authority over the waterway, using Trump's own framing to do it — while Araghchi separately said he had met Oman's foreign minister in Muscat to continue technical talks on jointly managing the strait, a process anticipated under the original U.S.-Iran memorandum.

The institutional pushback

The United Nations' International Maritime Organization moved quickly to reject the legal premise of Trump's proposal, regardless of who is charging it. A spokesperson said the agency "stands firmly against charging fees for passage through straits used for international navigation," adding there is "no legal basis through which to introduce mandatory tolls simply to transit through a strait." The statement applies with equal force to Tehran's past attempts to toll shipping and to Washington's new proposal — a rare instance of the same international body constraining both sides of the conflict on identical legal grounds.

Why it matters

Roughly a fifth of global oil and gas trade transits the Strait of Hormuz. A mandatory cargo-value levy of 20% — if that is in fact what is being proposed — would represent a fundamentally different order of magnitude from any existing chokepoint toll, with direct pass-through effects on global energy and freight costs. Even short of implementation, the announcement itself has already repriced risk: oil higher, equities lower, and vessel traffic down by more than half in three days.

The deeper significance may be rhetorical rather than logistical. By adopting the language of tolls and reimbursement — the same mechanism it spent months telling Iran was illegal — Washington has narrowed the distance between its position and Tehran's, complicating any future U.S. argument that Iran's toll claims are uniquely without legal basis. Araghchi's response was calibrated precisely to exploit that opening.

What to watch

  • Whether the Trump administration issues any formal mechanism, agency designation, or legal justification for the 20% fee, or whether it remains a Truth Social statement without operational follow-through.
  • Iran-Oman technical talks on strait management, which continue independent of the U.S. blockade announcement.
  • Shipping industry response — insurers, charterers and tanker operators will price transit risk and potential fee exposure into near-term freight rates regardless of whether Washington's proposal is ever formalized.
  • Further IRGC action against vessels using the U.S.-favored southern route near Oman, which Iran has called unsafe and which triggered last week's strikes.
