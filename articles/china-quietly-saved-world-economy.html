How China Quietly Saved the World Economy

When Iran shut the Strait of Hormuz, analysts predicted $200 oil and a global blackout. Neither happened. The reason wasn't luck — it was Beijing, and it wasn't kindness.

Four weeks into the Iran war, the global economy was supposed to be on its knees. It wasn't. Brent crude, which analysts at Macquarie and others had warned could reach $200 a barrel — nearly triple its pre-war level — never got there. Kpler's lead oil analyst, Matt Smith, put the market's confusion bluntly: "I would have expected prices to be above $200. It's crazy. Everyone is scratching their heads about this."

The disruption that triggered those forecasts was real, and historic. When Iran effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz following the U.S.-Israeli strikes that began on February 28, it took roughly 20 million barrels a day — about a fifth of global oil supply — out of a market with zero slack. It was, by multiple analysts' accounts, the largest single oil-supply disruption in history.

The math should not have worked out. It did. And the biggest reason, largely unannounced and barely covered outside specialist energy circles, was China.

The buffers that weren't enough

The world did not sit still. Two alternate pipelines — Saudi Arabia's East-West "Petroline" and the UAE's Abu Dhabi crude pipeline — added back a combined 7 million barrels a day, routing around the strait entirely. A week into the war, the International Energy Agency's 32 member countries agreed to release 400 million barrels of oil from strategic reserves — the largest coordinated release in the organization's 50-year history, more than double the 182.7 million barrels released during the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine response. The United States alone contributed 172 million barrels, or 43% of the total, from its Strategic Petroleum Reserve.

Even so, the IEA's own director, Fatih Birol, was candid about the limits: a single release of that scale would offset the lost flow from Hormuz for roughly 20 days. Analysts at Rapidan Energy Group put the group's realistic sustained release rate at closer to 2 million barrels a day — nowhere near enough to cover a 20-million-barrel hole.

There was a second failure that most coverage missed: Saudi Arabia's spare capacity, the world's traditional insurance policy against oil shocks for half a century, turned out to be useless in this specific crisis. Riyadh had 2-3 million barrels a day of spare production capacity sitting idle. But spare capacity that cannot physically leave the Gulf is worthless — and with Hormuz closed to tanker traffic, roughly half of Saudi output sat stranded at the wellhead. OPEC+ raised its output targets in the following months, but those increases stayed "largely on paper" because the closure capped what Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Iraq could actually ship out.

So: alternate pipelines covered about a third of the gap. Emergency reserves covered a sliver more, and only briefly. Saudi Arabia's safety net failed on delivery, not supply. That left roughly 10-13 million barrels a day of deficit still unaccounted for — and that gap is where China enters the story.

The import collapse

China is the world's largest oil importer, and half of everything it buys transits the Strait of Hormuz. By any conventional logic, it should have been scrambling to secure alternative barrels at any price. Instead, its imports fell off a cliff.

Data compiled by the commodities intelligence firm Kpler shows Chinese seaborne crude arrivals dropping from 11.39 million barrels a day in February — the last full month before the war — to 8.10 million in April, 6.36 million in May, and roughly 6.4 million in June, the lowest level since October 2016. That is a collapse of nearly 45% inside four months, executed with no public announcement and no explanation from Beijing.

The mechanics, pieced together by analysts rather than confirmed by Chinese authorities, involved several moving parts. Beijing quietly banned its refiners from exporting refined fuel — a decision reported by sources "with knowledge of the matter" roughly two weeks into the war, which kept Asian neighbors like Vietnam and the Philippines short of fuel even as it preserved supply at home. Chinese refiners cut processing rates to record lows and leaned on refinery-held inventories rather than fresh imports, according to Kpler. Coal-fired power generation and coal-based petrochemical substitution increased. Gasoline and diesel sales posted double-digit declines in April, helped along by China's rapidly electrifying vehicle fleet — EVs made up roughly a quarter of all vehicles on the road by May, up a third from a year earlier.

But the biggest single factor was the one nobody can measure precisely: China simply drew down a reserve stockpile it had spent years quietly building.

A "Great Wall of Oil"

China does not publish its strategic reserve figures. Analysts instead track it by satellite — measuring shadows cast by the floating roofs of above-ground storage tanks, and increasingly using synthetic-aperture radar to gauge fill levels more precisely. Estimates before the war put China's visible crude storage at roughly 1.1 to 1.3 billion barrels, more than every other country's published reserve combined. Goldman Sachs strategist Kinger Lau called it a "Great Wall of Oil" and estimated it was enough to sustain the country for more than 110 days even with zero imports. That figure almost certainly understates the true total: China also stores oil in refinery tank farms and underground caverns that are far harder to observe from orbit.

A meaningful share of that stockpile, according to a tracking report from the Atlantic Council, was built using sanctioned Iranian and Russian crude — oil that Washington and Brussels had cut off from the dollar-based global market after 1996 and 2022, respectively. China was able to buy it anyway, using dark-fleet tankers operating outside normal maritime tracking, rebranding cargoes as Malaysian in origin, and settling payments in yuan rather than dollars — bypassing the U.S. financial system that gives Washington leverage over who can buy sanctioned oil in the first place. Because China was effectively the only large, willing buyer of that oil, it also bought it at a steep discount.

Not altruism — economics

The obvious question is why. And the least persuasive answer is the one that got the most attention: that Beijing did this to rescue the global economy, or even the United States, its principal strategic rival.

Reuters columnist Clyde Russell was blunt in dismissing that framing directly: the collapse in China's imports helped Asia adjust to a loss of at least 10 million barrels a day of crude, but that outcome was "a serendipitous side effect rather than any altruism on Beijing's part, which is responding to changing price and supply dynamics." China had built its reserve capacity over the prior two decades specifically to insulate itself from exactly this kind of shock — and once oil prices spiked, drawing down existing, cheaply acquired stockpiles rather than buying fresh barrels at $90-plus was simply the rational move. Notably, China did not resume buying even as prices eased toward the $70s later in the war — behavior inconsistent with a country that was managing price risk and nothing more, and more consistent with a country restocking a strategic buffer built for a specific future contingency.

That contingency has a name in Beijing's own strategic vocabulary: the "Malacca dilemma" — the scenario in which the U.S. Navy, in any conflict over Taiwan, closes the Malacca Strait and cuts off the roughly 80% of China's oil that transits it. Building a stockpile large enough to run the country for a year without imports is the single most effective hedge against that scenario. Iran's closure of Hormuz gave China an unplanned, real-world stress test — and it passed.

Two secondary motives likely reinforced the decision. China's export-dependent economy stood to lose enormous demand if a prolonged oil shock tipped its customers — Europe especially — into recession; absorbing the shock for three months preserved those markets. And Bloomberg columnist Javier Blas has argued that China's willingness and ability to flip roughly 5% of global oil demand on and off functions as a demonstration of a new kind of leverage — comparable to the "Arab oil weapon" of 1973 — over any country that buys or sells oil at scale.

Where this leaves India

For Indian readers, the immediate relevance is exposure, not admiration. India imports roughly 85% of its crude, with close to 40% historically routed through the Strait of Hormuz. Unlike China, India's dedicated Strategic Petroleum Reserve — 5.33 million tonnes across three coastal cavern sites — covers only about 9.5 days of net crude imports at full capacity. New Delhi's Petroleum Ministry maintained through the crisis that total national cover, including commercial and refinery stocks, stood closer to 74 days on paper and roughly 60 days in practice — comfortable in the near term, but a fraction of China's multi-hundred-day cushion, and well short of the International Energy Agency's 90-day benchmark for member states. India leaned instead on diversification — sharply increasing discounted Russian crude purchases, which rose from roughly 0.2% of imports in 2022 to about 20% by February 2026 — and a temporary U.S. sanctions waiver permitting continued Russian oil purchases while global supply adjusted.

The bigger picture

Two things changed permanently in the space of a few months, regardless of what motivated Beijing. First, the fifty-year assumption that Saudi Arabia's spare capacity guarantees global oil security has been shown to fail precisely when a chokepoint, not total supply, is the constraint — a vulnerability that applies to any future Gulf disruption, not just this one. Second, China has now publicly — if unofficially — demonstrated that it can absorb or release roughly 5% of world oil demand at will. Whether that was strategy, coincidence, or a hedge that happened to pay a dividend, the capability itself is now proven and cannot be un-seen. The next time Beijing's reserves move sharply in either direction, markets, and governments in New Delhi and Washington alike, will be watching far more closely than they were this time.
