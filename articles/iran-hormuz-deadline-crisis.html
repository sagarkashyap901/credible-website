Trump: US Will 'Destroy' Iran If I'm Assassinated

President says 1,000 missiles are aimed at Tehran as Washington gives Iran until Saturday to renounce attacks on shipping in the strait.

The ceasefire that ended the twelve-day war between Israel, the United States and Iran was already fraying by the time President Donald Trump sat down to write a Truth Social post late Friday night. What he posted next turned a regional shipping dispute into the most dangerous 72 hours in the US-Iran relationship since Operation Epic Fury.

By Saturday, Washington had drawn a hard line over the Strait of Hormuz. Two American carrier strike groups had moved within range of Iranian missiles. And the president of the United States had publicly confirmed he'd ordered the military to erase an entire country if Iran's government moved against him.

Here is what actually happened, verified against primary reporting, and what it signals for the region.

The threat: "1,000 missiles are locked and loaded"

In a Truth Social post published late Friday, Trump wrote that a thousand missiles were locked and loaded and aimed at Iran, with thousands more ready to follow if Tehran acted on threats to assassinate him. He said the order had already been given: the US military stood ready for a full year, extendable, to completely decimate and destroy every part of Iran. Bloomberg confirmed the post's language verbatim, reporting Trump had vowed the US military would carry out this destruction if Iranian leaders attempted or executed his assassination.

This was not an isolated outburst. The Hill's reporting traces a consistent pattern going back to a February 2025 executive order, in which Trump has repeatedly said his standing instructions to US officials, in the event of his assassination by Iran, are to obliterate the country. He has separately warned that Iran would be "blown off the face of the earth" if ceasefire talks collapsed and Hormuz attacks continued.

The timing was not incidental. The post landed two days after huge crowds gathered in Mashhad for the burial of former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, who was killed in a targeted strike on his Tehran compound on February 28 during the earlier war. Mourners at the funeral processions were filmed carrying placards calling for Trump's death, though Iran's government has made no public threat against him.

The intelligence behind the threat

The assassination fear itself has a documented, if contested, intelligence trail. CNN reported that Israel shared intelligence with the Trump administration reflecting a desire among elements of Iran's hardline leadership to target Trump, describing it as a broader sentiment rather than a confirmed, specific operational plot. The Wall Street Journal had earlier reported that Israel warned Washington about an alleged Iranian plot on Trump's life, without disclosing further detail.

This is not new territory. US intelligence officials reportedly gathered evidence by 2024 suggesting Tehran was exploring ways to kill then-candidate Trump, and his 2024 campaign said it had been briefed on specific threats. Iran's government has consistently and publicly denied any assassination plan, with President Masoud Pezeshkian previously dismissing similar claims as fear-mongering designed to worsen relations.

Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi did not address the assassination claim directly but hit back hard at Trump's rhetoric, criticizing what he called derogatory language and accusing Washington of breaching the memorandum of understanding the two sides signed weeks earlier.

The real trigger: a Saturday deadline over Hormuz

The missile threat, dramatic as it was, sat inside a narrower and more immediate crisis: a US ultimatum over the Strait of Hormuz.

Axios reported Friday, citing three US officials briefed to reporters, that the Trump administration had delivered a demand to Tehran, both directly and through regional mediators, to do three things by Saturday: publicly acknowledge the strait remains open to international shipping, commit to halting attacks on commercial vessels, and confirm passage would remain toll-free. Washington's position was blunt. One official told Axios they wanted Iran to explicitly or implicitly admit it had erred; a second warned there would be real consequences if Tehran refused, saying bluntly that it would not be a good day for them if it did.

The backdrop: Iran had fired on commercial vessels in and around Hormuz earlier in the week, which the US said violated the memorandum of understanding signed roughly three weeks prior. Washington responded with two rounds of strikes, and Iran retaliated with missile and drone fire against US-linked sites in Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar and Jordan. Trump had declared the ceasefire "over" on Wednesday, even as he simultaneously directed his administration to keep talking.

Axios also reported a striking behind-the-scenes detail, since corroborated by CBS News citing separate US officials: Iranian representatives privately told Washington the ship attacks were a mistake, attributing them to a rogue hardline faction inside the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps rather than official policy. Iran's foreign ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei publicly denied on Friday that Tehran had sought negotiations at all, so this remains Washington's characterization, not a fact both sides agree on.

Araghchi travelled to Muscat on Saturday and met Omani Foreign Minister Badr al-Busaidi, with discussions centered on transit mechanisms for the strait under Article 5 of the Islamabad memorandum, according to Araghchi's own account posted to Telegram. A high-level US delegation, including Vice President JD Vance, Secretary of State Marco Rubio, envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, was also dispatched to Muscat to engage regional mediators. As of Saturday's reporting, no full Iranian statement meeting Washington's exact demands had been confirmed publicly, meaning the deadline's actual enforcement remains an open question heading into this week.

The buildup: two carriers, twenty-plus warships

Diplomacy in Muscat was not happening in isolation. It was happening under the shadow of a fresh American naval surge.

The USS Abraham Lincoln and USS George H.W. Bush, both nuclear-powered carrier strike groups, moved into the Gulf of Oman, putting both within operational range of Iranian missiles. More than twenty US warships are now operating across the Middle East as part of the posture, with reporting indicating the Trump administration is actively weighing whether to reinstate a naval blockade on Iranian ports, a measure it briefly imposed earlier this year.

This is the second major buildup cycle in 2026. The first, in February, saw the arrival of the USS Gerald R. Ford alongside the Lincoln, a deployment analysts at the time called one of the most significant regional force postures since the 2003 Iraq invasion. That buildup preceded Operation Epic Fury, the war that killed Khamenei and dozens of senior Iranian commanders. The current repositioning of Lincoln and Bush into the Gulf of Oman, timed almost exactly to Saturday's deadline, reads less like coincidence and more like calibrated leverage: pressure and diplomacy moving on parallel tracks, a pattern that has defined nearly every phase of this conflict since February.

Positioning two strike groups inside Iranian missile range while negotiators sit in Oman is a deliberate signal, not routine rotation.

What it means

Three things are worth separating clearly for anyone tracking this story.

First, the assassination threat and the missile warning are real, verified statements from Trump, but they describe a contingency, not an imminent strike order. There is no confirmed, specific Iranian plot; there is shared intelligence describing hardline sentiment.

Second, the Hormuz deadline is the more immediate flashpoint. It is a concrete, time-bound demand with a real diplomatic track in Muscat, and its outcome will shape whether this de-escalates or reignites strikes within days.

Third, the carrier movements suggest Washington is not treating this as rhetoric. Positioning two strike groups inside Iranian missile range while negotiators sit in Oman is a deliberate signal, not routine rotation.

For India, the stakes are direct. A fifth of the world's oil and gas moves through Hormuz. Any prolonged closure or blockade pushes energy costs higher for an economy that imports roughly 85 percent of its crude, and a wider war would complicate India's careful balancing act between Washington, Tehran and Gulf partners simultaneously.
