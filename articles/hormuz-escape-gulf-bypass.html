The Hormuz Escape: How Saudi Arabia and the UAE Are Rebuilding Gulf Oil Geography Around a Blocked Strait

How Saudi Arabia and the UAE are rebuilding Gulf oil geography around a blocked strait — one nation has already hit a wall, the other is spending billions to make sure it never does.

Iran's Revolutionary Guard shut the Strait of Hormuz again on July 11, declaring the waterway closed "until regional interference by the US ceases." It is at least the fourth time this year the world's most important oil chokepoint has flipped between open and closed. As of July 17, tracking firm PortWatch recorded just 10 vessel transits against a pre-crisis baseline of 88 a day — a roughly 90% collapse in traffic through a strait that normally carries a fifth of the world's oil and LNG.

The crisis, now in its fifth month since the US and Israel struck Iranian targets on February 28 and killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, has forced Gulf producers to stop treating chokepoint risk as a contingency and start treating it as a permanent design constraint. Two countries are ahead of the rest: Saudi Arabia and the UAE. Their strategies, however, are pulling in opposite directions — one nation has maxed out a 40-year-old contingency plan with no further room to grow; the other is in the middle of building an entirely new export geography.

Saudi Arabia: a ceiling with no higher gear

Saudi Arabia's answer to Hormuz predates the current war by four decades. The 1,200-kilometer East-West Pipeline — known as Petroline — was authorized in 1979 during the Iran-Iraq War specifically as insurance against a Gulf blockade, running from the Abqaiq processing hub on the Persian Gulf coast to the Red Sea port of Yanbu.

For most of its history, Petroline carried a modest 1.7 to 2.8 million barrels a day. That changed after Houthi drone strikes hit Aramco's Abqaiq facility in 2019, prompting the company to prepare an emergency measure: converting a parallel natural-gas-liquids pipeline to crude service, raising theoretical capacity to 7 million barrels a day. That figure sat untested until this year.

On March 10, Aramco chief executive Amin Nasser told investors the company would hit full throughput within days. It did, on March 11. Bloomberg and other outlets covered the milestone; fewer outlets covered the bottleneck waiting on the other end. Yanbu's loading terminals can operationally handle roughly 4 to 4.5 million barrels a day under wartime conditions, according to estimates from Vortexa and Argus Media. Of the 7 million barrels moving through the pipeline, close to 2 million barrels are consumed by domestic refineries — SAMREF, YASREF and the Yanbu refinery — before any of it reaches a tanker.

The net result: five-day rolling crude departures from Yanbu have run between roughly 3.66 million and 5 million barrels a day, covering an estimated 50% to 70% of Saudi Arabia's pre-war export volumes, according to analyst estimates cited by Pipeline Technology Journal and Engineering News-Record. An April drone strike on one of the pipeline's eleven pumping stations briefly cut throughput by 700,000 barrels a day; the Saudi energy ministry confirmed full capacity was restored within days.

"7 million barrels a day is Petroline's absolute physical ceiling — there is no third pipeline, no further conversion available."Amin Nasser, CEO, Saudi Aramco

Saudi Arabia is also not fully free of chokepoint risk: cargo loaded at Yanbu still has to clear Bab el-Mandeb near Yemen, trading a bigger risk for a smaller one. Riyadh is reportedly holding early-stage talks with Kuwait, Bahrain and Qatar about routing some of their stranded volumes through Saudi pipeline capacity — though Petroline's own ceiling limits how much room actually exists for that.

The UAE: rebuilding, not patching

Where Saudi Arabia converted an existing asset to its limit, the UAE is constructing new capacity from the ground up — across oil, refined products, and now, ports.

The Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline, running from the Habshan fields to Fujairah on the Gulf of Oman, has carried the UAE's entire Hormuz-independent oil trade since 2012. Its capacity has been pushed to roughly 1.8 million barrels a day, well short of the country's pre-war production of about 3.4 million barrels a day.

A second, parallel pipeline — the West-East Pipeline — was already on ADNOC's drawing board before the war, a $3 billion, 300-kilometer project first evaluated in 2023. The crisis turned a multi-year plan into an emergency build. On May 15, Abu Dhabi Crown Prince Sheikh Khaled bin Mohamed bin Zayed ordered ADNOC to accelerate delivery. By May 20, ADNOC chief executive Sultan Al Jaber said the pipeline was already 50% complete, targeting operational status in 2027. Once finished, it will roughly double the UAE's bypass capacity to somewhere between 3.3 and 3.6 million barrels a day. ADNOC has since gone further, announcing in June its first multi-fuel pipeline to Fujairah to protect exports of gasoline, diesel and jet fuel — products none of the existing crude pipelines can carry.

"We're moving toward having zero Hormuz dependency — regardless of whether it's open or not."Thani Al Zeyoudi, UAE Minister of Foreign Trade

This sits inside a wider structural bet. The UAE formally exited OPEC on May 1, freeing itself from production quotas as it targets 5 million barrels a day of production capacity by 2027.

The port play: DP World's defensive billions

The oil pipeline is only half of the UAE's bypass strategy. The other half is cargo — and it's moving even faster.

Jebel Ali, DP World's flagship container port inside the Persian Gulf and one of the largest in the world, saw activity fall 90% to 95% after Iran's February closure, according to the Financial Times. The hit is not abstract: Moody's projects DP World's 2026 earnings will fall to roughly $5.9 billion from $6.6 billion in 2025, a $700 million loss tied directly to the strait's closure. DP World alone contributes more than 10% of UAE GDP and roughly a third of Dubai's.

The Financial Times reported on July 14 that DP World is now in active talks with UAE officials to build an entirely new multipurpose port on the Fujairah coastline, plus a separate container terminal at Fujairah's existing harbor — both on the Gulf of Oman, roughly 70 nautical miles clear of the strait. A senior DP World official described the project as a defensive hedge in case conditions worsen further. The port could be operational within about 18 months, with an initial investment in the hundreds of millions of dollars, though the project's structure and financing are still being negotiated. DP World has said Jebel Ali itself won't be scaled down — its industrial ecosystem can't simply be relocated — but the crisis made clear the port needs an alternative it didn't have.

The three left behind

Not every Gulf producer has an exit. Iraq's oil production collapsed from 4.3 million barrels a day to under 1.5 million after the closure, with the Rumaila field alone losing 700,000 barrels a day; its reopened pipeline to Turkey moves a fraction of that back out, around 250,000 barrels a day. Iran's own bypass pipeline to Jask remains sanctioned and underused.

Three countries have no bypass infrastructure at all. Kuwait declared force majeure on its export contracts and, according to remarks by state oil company chief Sheikh Nawaf al-Sabah at the Atlantic Council Global Energy Forum, is now in talks with Saudi Arabia and the UAE simply to squeeze its own crude into their pipeline networks. Bahrain's Sitra refinery has taken repeated missile damage and has also invoked force majeure. Qatar is the most exposed of all: Iranian missiles struck the Ras Laffan LNG complex — the largest such facility on Earth — on March 18 and 19, knocking out roughly 17% of Qatar's LNG export capacity, with repairs expected to take three to five years. QatarEnergy declared force majeure on contracts to Italy, Belgium, South Korea and China days later. Qatar alone supplies about a fifth of global LNG trade, and the IEA has said no alternative route currently exists at scale for that volume.

Why this matters for India

For an Indian audience, the stakes aren't abstract. Roughly half of India's daily crude imports — around 52%, per S&P Global Commodities at Sea — normally transit Hormuz, sourced heavily from Iraq, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait and Qatar. New Delhi has responded by leaning harder into Russian barrels, which briefly regained near-record volumes with a temporary US sanctions waiver, alongside opportunistic Venezuelan and West African cargoes. The sharpest disruption has actually been in LPG, where roughly 90% of India's imports historically came from Gulf producers with little pre-existing alternative infrastructure; the US has stepped in as a substitute supplier, at a higher freight cost.

The bottom line

Saudi Arabia has proven its 1980s contingency plan works — and proven it has no second act. The UAE is doing something structurally different: building new pipeline capacity, new refined-product routes, and now a new port, all converging on the same short stretch of coastline outside the strait, backed by an explicit national goal of reducing Hormuz dependency to zero. Whether that goal is achievable by 2027 is still an open question. But of the seven Gulf exporters exposed to the strait, only two are actually building their way out of it — and only one is trying to make the exit permanent.
