Trump Weighs Widest Escalation Yet in Iran War, as Tehran Threatens a Second Front at Bab al-Mandeb

Options briefed to the president include seizing an Iranian oil island and bombing a nuclear site buried deeper than Fordow — as Iran signals it could use Houthi allies to shut a second global shipping chokepoint.

Nearly five months into the renewed US-Iran war, President Donald Trump is weighing the most consequential escalation of the conflict to date, according to a Wall Street Journal report published July 16 citing US officials. The options on the table — seizing Iranian territory, bombing a fortified nuclear tunnel complex, and expanding strikes onto energy infrastructure — arrive as Tehran simultaneously threatens to widen the war itself, dangling the closure of a second critical waterway, the Bab al-Mandeb Strait, on top of its blockade of Hormuz.

The dual escalation tracks — one American, one Iranian — mark a hinge point in a war that began in February and briefly appeared headed toward resolution before collapsing back into open conflict this week.

What the WSJ reported

According to the Journal, Trump convened a Situation Room meeting on Tuesday, July 14, to review three escalatory options with his top national security aides:

  1. Seizing Kharg Island — Iran's primary oil export terminal — along with other territory near the Strait of Hormuz, using US ground troops.
  2. Bombing "Pickaxe Mountain," a fortified underground tunnel complex tied to suspected covert Iranian nuclear work. The site is built 300 to 475 feet into granite — deeper than the Fordow and Natanz facilities the US and Israel struck last year — and is believed to remain incomplete.
  3. Expanding airstrikes more broadly across Iran, including onto energy infrastructure.

Trump has not made a final decision, and officials cautioned he has previously floated and then abandoned major threats, including an earlier plan to seize Kharg Island outright. Some aides believe the public discussion of these options is partly designed to pressure Tehran back to the table rather than signal an imminent order to execute them. Even so, Trump has been increasingly explicit in public: he told radio host Hugh Hewitt this week that the US intends to target Pickaxe Mountain, and at an industry event following the second wave of July 15 strikes, he framed the moment as a decision point between a settlement and finishing the conflict outright.

The Journal's own framing is stark: if Trump approves the ground option or the Pickaxe strike, it would open the most dangerous phase of the war so far — one that risks pulling the US deeper into the Middle East, spiking gasoline prices domestically, and complicating Republican messaging heading into the midterms.

Why the diplomatic track has stalled

The interim ceasefire and memorandum of understanding signed by Washington and Tehran in June has effectively collapsed. Iran has not agreed to surrender its nuclear stockpile, the core US demand, despite the interim arrangement offering Tehran the ability to resume billions of dollars in open-market oil sales. That gridlock is what has pushed Trump to request tougher options from his team, according to the Journal's sourcing.

Iran's Foreign Ministry, for its part, said this week it currently has no plans to return to negotiations, arguing Washington disregarded the ceasefire from the outset and would not honor any future agreement either.

Tehran's counter-escalation: a second chokepoint

Iran has not limited its response to conventional retaliation. The IRGC has said Hormuz will remain closed until, in its words, America's aggression ends, and has framed the region's energy exports in stark terms: either everyone gets to export, or no one does. Iran's forces claim to have struck US Fifth Fleet command and logistics infrastructure in Bahrain in the past 48 hours, in addition to missile and drone exchanges with US forces directly.

More significant for global markets is the emerging threat to Bab al-Mandeb, the strait linking the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden. A senior adviser to Iran's Supreme Leader, Ali Akbar Velayati, has said the broader "axis of resistance" — the Iran-aligned network that includes the Houthis — has the capability to lock both waterways simultaneously. A Houthi political bureau official went further, warning that if tensions with Saudi Arabia keep escalating, both straits could close in what he called an operational alliance, which he said could send oil toward $200 a barrel.

Either everyone gets to export, or no one does.

Analysts at the Center for Strategic and International Studies have described the Bab al-Mandeb card as Iran's reserve "nuclear option" — one Tehran would only deploy if it concluded a return to all-out war was unavoidable. Notably, the Houthis have kept this threat "in abeyance" through most of the current war, preferring calibrated pressure over a full blockade that would invite direct international naval intervention. What has changed this week is that Houthi forces broke a four-year truce with Saudi Arabia, firing missiles at the kingdom after accusing it of striking Sanaa's airport — a shift that threatens Saudi Arabia's own Red Sea export route, which has been absorbing nearly half of Riyadh's crude exports as a Hormuz workaround.

The market is already pricing in the risk

Brent crude has traded near $85 a barrel this week, its highest level in a month, after climbing for four consecutive sessions and reversing roughly a third of its second-quarter decline that followed June's now-collapsed interim peace deal. Prices eased slightly to around $84.6–$84.8 on Thursday as traders booked profits, but the structural risk premium remains: the IMF flagged in a July 15 note that the strategic reserve buffers that cushioned the market during the war's first phase have now been drawn down, leaving less cushion against a fresh supply shock.

Scenario modeling circulated by energy analysts this week frames the tail risk bluntly: a simultaneous closure of both Hormuz and Bab al-Mandeb would put roughly a quarter of global oil supply and close to a third of global container shipping at risk at once, a disruption with no direct historical precedent — with price trajectories in that scenario running as high as $150–$200 a barrel.

Why this matters for India

For an Indian audience, the stakes run through energy security and inflation, not abstract geopolitics. India imports the overwhelming majority of its crude, and a substantial share transits Hormuz. Sustained Brent prices near $85, let alone a spike toward $150–$200 in a dual-chokepoint scenario, would flow directly into India's import bill, the rupee, and eventually retail fuel prices — the same transmission chain that hit Indian markets during the February-to-June phase of this war. A Bab al-Mandeb disruption compounds this by squeezing an alternate route Gulf exporters have been using to route around Hormuz, meaning India would face a shrinking set of stable supply corridors on both its western and southwestern maritime approaches simultaneously.

What to watch next

  • Whether Trump authorizes the Pickaxe Mountain strike or the Kharg Island option — either would be a clear signal the diplomatic track is dead for now.
  • Whether the Houthis convert their Bab al-Mandeb rhetoric into actual interdiction attempts, which analysts say depends on explicit direction from Tehran rather than the Saudi-Houthi flare-up alone.
  • Oil price action around the $85–$90 band as a leading indicator of how seriously markets are pricing a wider war.
  • Iran's response if the US does strike infrastructure — Tehran's military has already promised a response it says will be "superior," not merely proportionate.
