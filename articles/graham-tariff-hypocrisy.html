India, China Face 100% Tariffs While Europe's Russian Gas Imports Hit a Record

The bill written in Lindsey Graham's memory targets the world's two largest buyers of Russian crude — even as the European Union imports Russian gas at an all-time high, protected by an exemption clause written into the same legislation.

Lindsey Graham died on the night of July 11, 71 years old, days after flying home from Kyiv. He had gone there to finalize an agreement with Volodymyr Zelensky on the legislative project that had consumed much of his final eighteen months in the Senate: a bill to punish any nation still bankrolling Russia's war machine through energy purchases. Richard Blumenthal, the Connecticut Democrat who co-authored the bill with him, later called it a breakthrough "reached shortly before his death."

Four days later, a bipartisan group of senators unveiled the revised text. What started as a blunt instrument aimed at the world's two largest buyers of Russian crude has been reshaped, in the week since Graham's death, into something more selective — and considerably more forgiving of Washington's own allies.

From 500% to 100% — and From Everyone to Somebody

The original Sanctioning Russia Act of 2025, introduced by Graham and Blumenthal that April with 84 Senate co-sponsors, proposed a flat 500% tariff on any country still buying Russian oil, gas, or uranium. For over a year it went nowhere, stalled by disputes over how much tariff authority to hand the President — a fight that intensified after the Supreme Court ruled in February 2026 that Trump's emergency-powers tariffs under IEEPA were unconstitutional, meaning any new tariff regime would need to come from Congress itself, not an executive order.

The version now moving through the Senate caps the penalty at 100% and narrows the target list to five countries on crude oil — China, India, Slovakia, Hungary, and Azerbaijan — reassessed every 180 days. A separate, largely symbolic top-five list exists for natural gas: China, France, Japan, Hungary, and Belgium. The gas list matters less than it appears to, because of a clause that exempts any country buying under 15% of Russia's total gas exports and demonstrably reducing that share — a threshold written precisely to shield France, Japan, and Belgium. China, whose gas purchases blow past that line, doesn't get the exemption. Neither, most analysts expect, will Japan and France ever actually pay it.

The Real Weight Falls on Two Countries

Strip away the symbolism and the numbers tell you where this bill actually bites. China and India together account for 86% of all Russian crude exports since the invasion began, according to the Centre for Research on Energy and Clean Air (CREA), whose Kpler-based tracking is the dataset most cited by Reuters and the Financial Times on this question. In June 2026 alone, the two countries spent a combined $9.4 billion on Russian crude — China at roughly $4.9 billion, India at $4.5 billion, with India's Russian imports hitting an all-time monthly high, up 34% from May.

Slovakia, Hungary, and Azerbaijan round out the crude list for very different reasons than China and India. Hungary and Slovakia are landlocked EU states that receive Russian crude almost exclusively through the Druzhba pipeline, under an exemption Brussels itself carved into its own 2022 sanctions regime. Azerbaijan is a different case entirely: its state oil company, SOCAR, owns a refinery in Turkey — STAR — that has sourced up to 98% of its crude from Russia's Lukoil, refines it, and ships the products onward labeled as Azerbaijani or Turkish fuel. By early 2024, that single refinery was the fourth-largest recipient of Russian seaborne crude in the world, behind only two Chinese and two Indian refineries. It is less an energy dependency than an active sanctions workaround.

Turkiye: Bigger Buyer, No Tariff

The starkest omission from the bill is Turkiye, a NATO member that buys more Russian oil products (26% of global exports) than any other country and roughly 30% of Russia's pipeline gas — a bigger share than most of the countries actually named. Unlike India, Turkiye never joined Western sanctions on Russia at all.

Two forces appear to explain the pass. First, mechanically: Turkiye's Russian crude imports have been falling as its refineries diversify toward Norwegian, Iraqi, and Guyanese supply, even as its refined-products and pipeline-gas purchases stay high — and the bill's crude list counts crude specifically. Second, and more decisively, politically: Turkiye controls the Bosphorus and Dardanelles under the Montreux Convention, the only route for NATO warships into the Black Sea, and hosts the critical Incirlik air base. At last week's NATO summit, Trump signaled he may lift CAATSA sanctions on Turkey's defense procurement agency and revive its place in the F-35 program, seven years after Ankara was expelled over its S-400 purchase. Threatening Turkiye with a tariff bill in the same week Washington is courting it back into the F-35 program would undercut its own diplomacy.

Europe's Own Ledger

The bill's authors have built in more protection for Western allies than the topline numbers suggest is warranted. The European Union imported roughly $451 million of Russian crude and $1.59 billion of Russian gas in June 2026 alone — an annualized run-rate of nearly $24.4 billion a year on oil and gas combined. Just Yamal LNG shipments to the EU hit an all-time high of €5.96 billion ($6.82 billion) in the first half of 2026, a 16% year-on-year increase, with EU buyers absorbing more than 97% of that single facility's output.

The EU has also quietly increased its imports of Russian enriched uranium, spending $185.9 million between January and April 2026 alone — nearly eight times the $20.7 million spent in the same period a year earlier, according to Eurostat data. France continues receiving both natural and reprocessed enriched uranium from Russia's Rosatom; customs records show Russian uranium's share of French imports fell from 67% in 2022 to 24% in 2024, but Greenpeace France documented deliveries continuing into 2025, including a shipment at Dunkirk that October.

None of this is illegal under EU rules — natural gas and nuclear fuel were deliberately left off Brussels' own sanctions list. But it complicates the moral clarity of a bill built around punishing "buyers of Russian energy," when the bloc writing the loudest condemnations is itself running near-record Russian gas and uranium volumes.

Japan's Standing Waiver

Japan presents the cleanest case of a double standard, because its exemption isn't even part of this bill — it's older, and it's American. The US Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control has repeatedly renewed a sanctions waiver allowing Japan to keep importing crude and LNG from the Sakhalin-2 project, majority-owned by Gazprom, most recently extended through June 2026. Japan's Minister of Economy, Trade and Industry, Ryosei Akazawa, called Russian crude "extremely important" for the country's energy security in March, as Middle East supply lines came under strain from the Hormuz conflict.

The dollar figures are modest by comparison — Japan's Russian crude imports totaled just $32.59 million for all of 2025, essentially a rounding error. But Japan's Russian gas purchases are not: recalculated from Russia's own reported global LNG revenue and Japan's tracked 18-19% share of that market, Japan's actual Russian gas spend runs closer to $4.3 billion a year — not the smaller figure a simple contract-volume estimate would suggest, since Japan appears to be absorbing spot-market Russian LNG beyond its fixed Sakhalin-2 allocation, particularly cargo that the EU turned away in April.

The Loophole Inside the Loophole

Even where the EU has banned Russian oil products outright — a rule that took effect January 21, 2026 — Russian crude keeps arriving indirectly. Refineries in India, Turkiye, Brunei, and Georgia that run substantially on Russian feedstock exported $880 million worth of oil products to sanctioning countries in June 2026 alone; CREA estimates roughly $399 million of that was genuinely Russian-crude-derived. Eight such shipments reached EU ports that month despite the ban — four from Turkish refineries, two from India, two from Georgia — landing in France, Italy, Bulgaria, Belgium, Cyprus, and Malta.

India's Jamnagar refinery, owned by Reliance Industries, ran on roughly 27% Russian crude over the prior three months; Turkiye's Tupras Izmit refinery ran on around 60%. In June, the UK took its first-ever shipment of jet fuel from Jamnagar — valued at about $68 million — under a new UK exemption specifically written to permit diesel and jet fuel refined from Russian crude. A Congressional Fix With Staying Power

One detail elevates this bill above Trump's earlier, more improvised tariff threats: because the Supreme Court's February ruling stripped the President of emergency-powers tariff authority, this penalty would exist as an actual act of Congress. A future administration couldn't undo it by simply rescinding an emergency declaration — it would require Congress to act again. That durability is precisely why Senate Majority Leader John Thune has called passing it "a fitting" tribute to Graham, even while stopping short of committing to a floor vote, and why sponsors are targeting passage by August.

Not everyone in Washington is convinced it will work as intended. Catherine Wolfram, a former Treasury official, has called using tariffs as a sanctions tool untested, and doubts India will simply comply. Senator Rand Paul has separately argued the bill risks damaging trade relationships far beyond its intended targets. Trump himself wants to expand the bill's scope to include Iran and Hezbollah — an addition Blumenthal is resisting, for fear it stalls the whole package in the House.

What the Numbers Actually Say

Line up every buyer's dollar figures side by side, and the bill's logic gets harder to defend as anything but selective. China and India's combined Russian energy purchases dwarf everyone else's by a full order of magnitude. But the EU's own annualized $24-26 billion in Russian oil, gas, and uranium, and Japan's roughly $4.3 billion, sit entirely outside the bill's actual reach — protected by a threshold clause, a decades-old NATO relationship, and a rolling Treasury waiver, respectively.

Lindsey Graham built this bill to punish anyone still financing Vladimir Putin's war. What's moving through the Senate in his name punishes two of Russia's customers and quietly writes exemptions for the rest.

Sourcing: Centre for Research on Energy and Clean Air (CREA), Eurostat, Kpler, Reuters, Financial Times, Greenpeace France, and US Treasury (OFAC) records, alongside on-record statements from named senators and officials. Dollar figures reflect the periods specified. This analysis distinguishes legally permitted purchases from sanctions circumvention where the two differ.
