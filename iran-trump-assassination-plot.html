Iran Weighed Fresh Plot to Kill Trump, Israeli Intelligence Warns, as Tehran Ties Fray

A new Israeli intelligence warning about an Iranian plot to assassinate Trump arrives days after his NATO summit remarks — and deepens a rift between Washington and Jerusalem over how far to push the military campaign against Iran.

Israeli intelligence agencies have shared new information with Washington indicating that Iran developed a fresh plan to assassinate President Donald Trump, according to people familiar with the matter, marking what officials describe as a significant escalation in an already volatile standoff between the United States and Tehran.

The warning, first reported by The Wall Street Journal and separately confirmed by CNN, was delivered to US officials this week. It followed public remarks by Trump at the NATO summit in Ankara, where he told reporters that Iranian officials "want to take out the U.S. leader — me," adding that he was aware of being placed on multiple target lists compiled by Tehran.

The disclosure comes at a precarious moment. A 60-day ceasefire brokered after last month's conflict between the US, Israel and Iran has effectively collapsed, with both sides trading strikes and Iranian forces continuing to target commercial shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. Trump this week described the memorandum of understanding underpinning the truce as "over," even as diplomats on both sides continue lower-profile talks aimed at a longer-term nuclear settlement.

The warning

According to CNN's reporting, two US officials confirmed that Israel's warning this week concerned a specific, newly identified plot — distinct from what one official described as a "steady drumbeat" of lower-grade intelligence Washington has been receiving for weeks about possible attempts on Trump's life. Sources cautioned that the US had not independently verified the Israeli intelligence and had not been tracking the specific plot before Jerusalem raised it.

That caveat has fed a degree of skepticism inside parts of the American intelligence community. Some officials, speaking to CNN on condition of anonymity, characterized the timing of the Israeli disclosure as part of a broader effort by Jerusalem to influence Trump's calculus as he weighs whether to intensify US military action against Iran — a policy question that has put Trump at odds with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu in recent weeks. Netanyahu has pushed for sustained military pressure on Tehran; Trump has been more inclined to preserve space for a negotiated settlement.

The White House, asked for comment on the reported warning, pointed reporters to Trump's own remarks at the NATO summit rather than issuing a separate statement. The Israeli embassy in Washington declined to comment. Iran's mission to the United Nations did not respond to requests for comment from the outlets that first reported the story.

A recurring threat

This is not the first time Tehran has been accused of plotting against Trump.

  • November 2024: the US Justice Department charged an alleged Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps operative, Farhad Shakeri, with directing a plan to surveil and ultimately kill Trump ahead of his second term. Shakeri — an Afghan national who had previously served a 14-year US prison sentence before being deported — was later tracked to Iran. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth confirmed earlier this year that Shakeri had been killed, telling reporters that the operative who had "attempted to assassinate President Trump" had been "hunted down."
  • March 2026: a federal jury separately convicted a Pakistani national accused of ties to the IRGC in connection with a distinct conspiracy targeting Trump and other senior US officials.

Iranian officials have consistently linked the threat narrative to the January 2020 US drone strike in Baghdad that killed Qassem Soleimani, the influential commander of the IRGC's Quds Force. Tehran has for years framed retaliation for Soleimani's death as a matter of unfinished national business. During funeral proceedings last weekend for Iran's Supreme Leader, killed in the opening days of the current conflict, mourners in Tehran were seen chanting for Trump's death, with at least one banner reading "We Will Kill Trump," according to Reuters.

The Netanyahu call

Trump and Netanyahu spoke by phone on Thursday, according to a readout from the Israeli prime minister's office, with the two leaders agreeing to maintain close coordination between Washington and Jerusalem. Trump used the call to update Netanyahu on recent American operations in the Gulf, while Netanyahu raised concerns over the pending US sale of F-35 fighter jets to Turkey and sharply worded recent remarks by Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdogan regarding Israel's legitimacy. A White House official told Israeli outlet Walla that despite the call, no in-person meeting between the two leaders is currently planned.

Security posture

Trump, asked directly whether concerns over an assassination threat had influenced his decision to route home aboard an alternate Air Force One aircraft rather than the newer plane, rejected the suggestion, saying the aircraft had instead been sent to a US base in Europe for troops to view. He nonetheless acknowledged the risks inherent to the office.

"The life of a President is very dangerous. You should have told me that years ago. Maybe I wouldn't have run."

Separately, CNN has reported that US officials in recent weeks privately warned Iranian intermediaries of their own — flagging concern that Israel might move to assassinate senior Iranian officials involved in nuclear negotiations, including parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi. That warning, first reported by The New York Times, underscores how assassination threats have become a feature running in both directions of the current standoff, even as both governments maintain that backchannel diplomacy continues.

What it means

For now, Washington and Tehran remain locked in a dual-track posture: active military exchanges around the Gulf and Strait of Hormuz on one hand, and quiet diplomatic engagement toward a longer-term nuclear agreement on the other, with officials on both sides pointing to a mid-August target for renewed talks. The assassination warning adds a distinctly personal dimension to a conflict that has, until now, been fought largely through proxies, sanctions and airstrikes — and raises the stakes for how Trump himself may approach the coming weeks of decision-making on Iran.
