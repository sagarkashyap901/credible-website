The Two-Port Trap: How Sabang and Great Nicobar Are Quietly Closing In On China's Biggest Weakness

Modi's Jakarta visit produced more than photo-ops. A port deal, a missile package and an air-force upgrade now sit on either side of the world's most valuable chokepoint — and Beijing has no good answer to any of it.

On July 8, Narendra Modi stood beside Indonesian President Prabowo Subianto at the Istana Merdeka in Jakarta and signed off on what looked, on paper, like a routine bundle of bilateral agreements. Twenty memorandums of understanding, covering everything from critical minerals to election technology. But three of those agreements — taken together, not separately — amount to the most consequential Indian Ocean maneuver New Delhi has made in years: joint development of Sabang Port, an Astra air-to-air missile package for the Indonesian Air Force, and an expansion of India's BrahMos footprint in the archipelago.

Read individually, each is a transaction. Read as one document, they describe a strategy: India is positioning itself on both shoulders of the Strait of Malacca, arming the country that controls the strait's eastern shore, and doing it all while Beijing watches from a position it cannot easily contest.

This is the story of why that matters — and where the story gets more complicated than the headlines suggest.

The chokepoint that keeps Beijing's planners awake

To understand why a port deal in northern Sumatra is geopolitically significant, you have to understand what the Strait of Malacca actually is to China.

In 2003, then-President Hu Jintao told the Communist Party's Central Committee that China faced what he called the "Malacca Dilemma" — a single, narrow waterway through which the country's economic lifeline flowed, controlled by nobody in particular but chokeable by anybody with a navy. Two decades later, the dependency has only deepened. Roughly two-thirds of China's total seaborne trade and more than 80% of its oil imports transit the strait, a route so concentrated that it carries close to 30% of global seaborne oil trade — more than even the Strait of Hormuz. At its narrowest point, the strait is barely wider than a large container ship.

China has spent enormous sums trying to engineer its way out of this vulnerability: pipelines through Myanmar and Central Asia, the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor running overland from Gwadar to Xinjiang, and the broader "String of Pearls" network of ports from Hambantota to Djibouti. The results have been underwhelming. Even with roughly 3.7 million barrels a day of pipeline capacity built out, that is nowhere close to replacing the nearly 15 million barrels China consumes daily — and independent estimates suggest a forced rerouting around Malacca could cost China as much as $220 billion a year. Most of the country's energy still arrives the same way it did in 2003: by sea, through the same strait.

This is the vulnerability India has just placed two hands on.

The pincer: Sabang and Galathea Bay

Sabang sits at the northern tip of Sumatra, directly overlooking the western entrance to the Strait of Malacca — a deep-water port, capable of berthing vessels up to 40 meters in draft, including submarines. Under the Jakarta agreements, India and Indonesia have set up a joint task force to develop the port and the connectivity between it and India's Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

Roughly 160 kilometers away sits the other half of the pincer: Galathea Bay, the International Container Transshipment Port under construction on India's Great Nicobar Island, positioned just 40 nautical miles from the same strait entrance. Phase one, targeting 4 million TEUs of capacity, is slated for completion by 2028, backed by an outlay that has grown from roughly ₹72,000 crore to closer to ₹81,000 crore as the airport and township components have expanded. In February 2026, the National Green Tribunal cleared the project's remaining legal hurdles, and dredging tenders are now moving.

Put the two points on a map and the picture is unambiguous. No vessel — commercial or naval, above water or submerged — passes through the western mouth of Malacca without transiting a corridor now flanked by Indian-linked infrastructure on both sides.

This is not a blockade capability. India does not have the naval mass, nor the intent, to close the strait. What it has built is something more useful in peacetime: persistent presence, domain awareness, and the standing option to make the corridor harder to use unilaterally in any future crisis. That distinction — presence versus blockade — matters, and any serious analysis has to hold onto it rather than overstate what a port deal actually buys.

For India, there's a commercial payoff too, and it's not small. Currently, more than 75% of India's transshipped cargo routes through foreign hubs — Colombo, Singapore, Port Klang — before reaching its final destination. Great Nicobar alone is projected to save Indian shippers an estimated $200–220 million annually once operational, while shaving three to five days off transit times to Europe and Africa. Sabang extends that logic outward: a joint Indian footprint on Indonesian soil, at the edge of the world's busiest trade artery.

Why Indonesia said yes: Astra, BrahMos and a hedge against Beijing

The port deal didn't arrive alone. It came bundled with two missile agreements that reveal exactly what kind of security partner Jakarta thinks it needs right now.

Astra Mk-1, India's indigenous beyond-visual-range air-to-air missile, is headed to Indonesia's Sukhoi fleet — a mix of Su-27s and Su-30s that has run for years on aging Russian R-77 stock. If finalized, Indonesia becomes Astra's first international customer, a milestone Indian officials are treating as validation of the country's shift from arms importer to arms exporter. New Delhi's pitch leans heavily on the missile's use by IAF Su-30MKIs during Operation Sindoor in May 2025 — the first real combat data any Astra variant has generated.

BrahMos is the more established piece of the package. Indonesia has now signed on for coastal defence batteries, becoming the third Southeast Asian BrahMos operator after the Philippines (2022, three batteries, $375 million) and Vietnam. Reported figures for the Indonesian package vary — outlets have cited numbers from $200 million for two batteries to a scaled-up order Jakarta is reportedly pushing for beyond its original single-battery ask — but the direction is consistent: Jakarta wants more, not less, of a missile system with a combat-tested, largely unchallenged record from Operation Sindoor's opening strikes.

Why does Indonesia want either? The Natuna Islands are the honest answer. Jakarta has spent the better part of a decade in low-grade friction with Chinese coast guard vessels and maritime militia inside its own exclusive economic zone near Natuna — a zone China doesn't formally dispute but treats, in practice, as overlapping "traditional fishing grounds." Indonesian naval vessels have repeatedly shadowed and expelled Chinese coast guard ships from the area. And in a genuinely awkward wrinkle for Jakarta's stated non-claimant status, Prabowo's government also signed a joint maritime development pact with Beijing in late 2024 that legal experts criticized as an implicit acknowledgment of "overlapping claims" — a move that arguably undercuts Indonesia's own long-standing legal position under UNCLOS.

That contradiction is worth sitting with, because it's the real story of Indonesian strategy: Jakarta is not choosing India over China. It is hedging against both, extracting economic cooperation from Beijing while building deterrent hardware with New Delhi, Washington and others simultaneously. The Astra and BrahMos deals aren't a declaration of alignment — they're an insurance policy Jakarta is buying because it does not fully trust the Natuna arrangement to hold.

The part the headlines are skipping

An honest analysis has to include the pushback, and there is a real one. India's own defence commentary has flagged that the Astra Mk-1's Operation Sindoor record is more mixed than the export pitch suggests — its engagement range sits around 110 km, well short of the Chinese-origin PL-15E missiles Pakistan's air force fielded during the same conflict, and DRDO itself moved to incorporate lessons from a recovered PL-15E into the next-generation Astra Mk-2 (targeting roughly 240 km) precisely because the gap was evident.

The missile being sold to Indonesia is the Mk-1 — the same variant that prompted that reverse-engineering effort — not the more capable Mk-2, which isn't yet in production. BrahMos, by contrast, has a cleaner combat record from the same operation and faces no comparable credibility question. Conflating the two systems in a single "combat-proven" pitch is doing more work for India's export narrative than the facts fully support, and Indonesia — a sophisticated, sovereign buyer — is almost certainly aware of that distinction even as it signs.

None of this undercuts the port deal's significance. But it's the difference between reporting the story and selling it, and it's worth being precise about which missile is actually carrying the "combat-tested" label into this deal.

What Beijing can — and can't — do about it

China has no clean countermove here. It cannot contest Sabang without directly confronting Indonesian sovereignty, which would blow up the very "overlapping claims" arrangement it just extracted from Prabowo's government at Natuna. It cannot easily accelerate its own pipeline diversification fast enough to meaningfully close the Malacca gap within this decade. And every additional BrahMos battery across the Philippines, Vietnam and now Indonesia adds another node to what regional defence analysts are already calling a distributed anti-access network stretching across the South China Sea's southern and western approaches — each individually modest, collectively a change in the cost calculus for any Chinese naval movement through the region.

The likelier Chinese response isn't military escalation around Sabang itself. It's economic: deeper investment pushes into Indonesia to keep Jakarta's hedge from tilting further toward New Delhi and Washington, more assertive coast guard patrols near Natuna to remind Jakarta who has the bigger fleet, and continued diplomatic pressure to keep the ASEAN Code of Conduct negotiations from hardening against Chinese interests. Don't expect a confrontation at Sabang. Expect a quieter contest for Jakarta's loyalty to intensify.

The bottom line

This is not, as some framing suggests, India versus China in a straightforward zero-sum contest for a strait. It's more precise — and more interesting — than that. India has used a genuine commercial need (transshipment capacity, reduced dependency on foreign ports) and a genuine Indonesian security anxiety (Natuna, an under-modernized air force, a hedge against overreliance on Beijing) to place itself, quietly and legally, on both banks of the world's most economically important waterway. Great Nicobar was always going to happen regardless of Jakarta. Sabang is what turns a domestic infrastructure project into a regional strategic architecture.

Whether that architecture ever gets tested is a separate question, and probably a distant one. What's not in question is the geography: two ports, one strait, and a great power on the losing end of an equation it has been trying to solve since 2003.
